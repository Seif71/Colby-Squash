What is Squash?

Squash is a high-intensity indoor racquet sport played on a four-walled court where two players alternate striking a hollow rubber ball against the front wall, attempting to outmaneuver one another by placing shots that cannot be returned before the second bounce. According to the World Squash Federation (WSF), the sport demands exceptional aerobic capacity, strength, agility, and tactical decision-making, as rallies involve continuous movement, rapid changes of direction, and precise control of shot angles and tempo. Modern squash uses the international scoring format known as "point-a-rally" to 11, with players serving from designated service boxes and seeking positional dominance around the central "T," which allows optimal court coverage.

The roots of squash trace back to the early 19th century at Harrow School in London, where students adapted the older game of rackets and discovered that softer balls created more dynamic rebound patterns off the wallsâ€”a development documented in Harrow's historical archives and widely referenced by the WSF and PSA. Throughout the 20th century, squash expanded internationally, becoming a major competitive sport across Europe, North America, the Middle East, and Asia.

The Professional Squash Association (PSA) now governs the global professional tour, and major competitions such as the World Squash Championships, Commonwealth Games, and Pan American Games feature the world's top athletes. Media outlets like BBC Sport and Forbes frequently highlight squash as one of the most physically demanding sports due to its combination of speed, endurance, and complex strategy. Today, squash is played in more than 180 countries and remains one of the world's most competitive and respected racquet sports, supported by a global infrastructure of clubs, national federations, and international governing bodies. History of Colby Squash

Colby Squash began in the 1960s, when the college first built its original squash courts and officially launched a varsity team. In those early decades, the program competed mostly against other New England colleges such as Bowdoin, Bates, Trinity, Tufts, and Williams, building a strong regional presence and a loyal community around the sport. Throughout the 1970s, 80s, and 90s, the team steadily developed through dedicated coaching, motivated athletes, and a culture that valued both competitiveness and camaraderie. Even without major facilities, Colby earned respect within the College Squash Association and regularly appeared in national postseason play, with multiple seasons in the Summers Cup and Conroy Cup divisions.

The most dramatic shift came in 2020 with the opening of the Harold Alfond Athletics and Recreation Center a transformative, world-class athletic facility that completely changed the trajectory of the program. The center introduced nine international squash courts, modern training technology, and a recruiting environment that immediately elevated Colby's ability to attract top talent. Coaches and players often describe this moment as the beginning of a "new era" for the program. With stronger athletes, better resources, and a renewed sense of ambition, Colby Squash climbed the national rankings and reached as high as #13 in the College Squash Association standings, the highest ranking in program history.

Today, squash holds a unique place at the college as one of only two Division I sports at Colby, alongside men's ice hockey. The program has evolved from a small regional team into a nationally recognized contender with growing momentum, deeper roster depth, and a long-term vision that continues to raise the bar. With its rich history, upgraded facilities, and rising competitive success, Colby Squash stands as one of the college's most exciting and fast-growing athletic programs.

Why Does Egypt Dominate Squash?

Egypt's dominance in global squash can be attributed to a combination of cultural, structural, and developmental factors documented across several reliable sources. The country maintains one of the world's strongest squash ecosystems, with major clubs such as Al Ahly, Heliopolis, Gezira, and Wadi Degla providing year-round training and high-frequency junior tournaments that give young athletes significantly more competitive exposure than their counterparts abroad (BBC Sport, 2019).

Egyptian coaching methodology further reinforces this success; as reported by The Atlantic, coaches emphasize an attacking, creative, and instinctive playing style that encourages players to take initiative rather than rely on conservative tactics (Gerard, 2017). Professional Squash Association (PSA) interviews with Egyptian coaches also highlight a development system that rewards risk-taking and adaptability, producing technically versatile and mentally resilient athletes.

Additionally, squash in Egypt is relatively affordable and widely accessible, expanding the talent pipeline compared with countries where the sport is concentrated in expensive private clubs (The Guardian, 2018). This environment, combined with the influence of generational role models such as Amr Shabana, Ramy Ashour, Nour El Sherbini, Ali Farag, and Mohamed ElShorbagy, creates a self-reinforcing cycle in which elite performers inspire rising players, leading Egypt to sustained international dominance. Their strategy relies heavily on instinct: Egyptian players are trained to trust their feel for the game rather than follow rigid patterns. They also mix pace constantly fast, slow, stop, explode which prevents opponents from settling into a rhythm.

Another key strategy is controlling the "T" through early preparation and quick movement, allowing them to dominate the middle and dictate rallies. Overall, their approach is fast, fearless, unpredictable, and designed to force errors and create opportunities rather than react to the opponent.

What Colby Squash Can Learn from Egyptian Teams?

According to reporting from BBC Sport (2019) and The Atlantic (2017), Egyptian squash players succeed because they train in an environment that encourages creativity, attacking play, and instinctive decision making rather than overly rigid structure. Colby Squash can learn from this by adopting a more aggressive style that emphasizes early volleying, taking time away from opponents, and using deceptive shot selection traits consistently highlighted in PSA interviews with Egyptian world champions such as Ramy Ashour, Ali Farag, and Nour El Sherbini. Egyptian players also gain an advantage through high-frequency match play; juniors compete almost weekly, which builds composure under pressure and tactical flexibility. This principle suggests that Colby athletes could benefit from increasing match-based drills, internal competitions, and pressure scenarios in training. Furthermore, The Guardian's analysis of Egyptian squash development emphasizes the importance of role-model effects: young Egyptians grow up watching national champions, studying their style, and adopting their confident mentality. Colby can mirror this by integrating more film analysis of top PSA matches, encouraging players to model elite patterns of movement, and creating a team culture that rewards creativity, quick adaptation, and taking smart risks. These evidence-based lessons provide a clear roadmap for strengthening both the tactical and psychological aspects of the Colby Squash program.
